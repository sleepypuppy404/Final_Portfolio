Context

Amongst the medical field, one of the biggest issues it faces is documentation. How can a doctor record notes while simultaniously giving the patient the attention they deserve. How can I cleanly keep everything organized per patient, and not interrupt their care. Making sure doctors have both a way to keep their focus exclusively on the patient is both an important social and technical problem. From this question came the YCombinator startup: Knowtex, which aims to utilize AI voice technology to create a note taking software for doctors.
Click to visit Knowtex's YCombinator page.






Getting Started


Because this project combines both voice to note functionality and medical note organization, and we were unable to look at Knowtex's website, I figured the best place to start was looking at software that accomplished similar goals to what KnowTex aimed to.

Medical Organization Software

When looking at medical organization software, one pre-existing programs stood out to me. AthenaOne, which combined health record, revenue management, and patient engagement. This ended up being a functionality that we ended up including within our version of the KnowTex product

Initial Designs

On this project, I worked with a team of 4 other UIUX designers. In order to explore more options for the applications and components necessary to build software like Knowtex, we each created sketches of different application pages. Following this, we created a single unified wireframe of what we believed the prototype should look like. Some design considerations we made were:
  • Combining the calendar layout as well as the patient display page, but creating separate tabs for each section.
  • Utilizing the display style for the visit records shown in the fourth set of sketches, but the overall structure and layout we used came from the second set of sketches
  • We chose to include a separate calibration page to make this functionality clear
  • We displayed the patient information on each of the relevant pages for a specific patient's records for a user to be able to easily keep track of which patient the record corresponds to.

Iterative Design Process

Prototype 1

After putting together the above wireframe and sketches, the next step was to create a Figma Mockup of the prototype. The goal of this prototype was to be critiqued by other students, and help us create our final mockup from this prototype.

Check out the prototype below or click on the link to check it out in Figma

Critique of Prototype 1

After finishing the initial first prototype, the next step was to get feedback from other users. In order to do so, we went to a weekly student design studio, and presented our prototype. Students were able to play with our prototype, then provide us useful feedback, which is described below:
  • Participants were split on whether adding symbols to buttons could make the page less dense and easier to navigate, or whether keeping the buttons with text labels gave the application a more official, medical feel.
  • Participants suggested that it would be helpful to be able to access the voice calibration page from other pages (such as in the middle of a visit, in case the system stops working).
  • Participants noted that the locations of buttons felt unintuitive or unpredictable, with “back” buttons appearing in different locations on different pages.
  • Some feedback was given about editing or deleting past records: however, the reality of the healthcare system is that once records have been published to a patient’s medical record, they may not be altered or removed.

Finalized Product

Final Prototype

After receiving the feedback from above, we decided to make a new prototype. To address the feedback, we made the following changes:
  • We added some symbols to certain buttons (like the log out button), but largely decided to keep — but simplify — the text on buttons, hopefully keeping the same formal feel while making it faster to navigate.
  • We added a “re-calibrate voice recognition” button to the record in-progress page.
  • We shifted the locations of buttons to hopefully clarify flow, while keeping the “back” button in the top right of every screen.
  • We added warnings to the interface to more clearly specify to users that once published, records cannot be edited or deleted.

User Testing

After finalizing our prototype, we wanted to get some eyes on it from users who knew nothing about what we were doing. To do so, we uploaded the prototype to a video testing platform, and created the following testing sequence for users:
  1. Calibrate the live voice transcription system
  2. Create a new visit record for John Dough’s 9-10am visit on Thursday, May 5th
  3. Edit and save the Service & Code form (SC form for short)
  4. Complete and publish the Visit Record and Service & Code forms to John Dough’s medical record
The video user tests are also available below

Testing Videos

User Feedback

After receiving the feedback, here are the changes recommended for the next iteration.
  1. Make page locations more intuitive
  2. Bold less items, as it can be distracting
  3. Interface not intuitive for first time users
  4. Give more task confirmation
  5. Simplify the interface overall.
